                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Franklin-D-Roosevelt">
                         Franklin D. Roosevelt
                        </a>
                        and
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Harry-S-Truman">
                         Harry S. Truman
                        </a>
                        (United States). The
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Axis-Powers">
                         Axis powers
                        </a>
                        were led by
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adolf-Hitler">
                         Adolf Hitler

                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Benito-Mussolini">

                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Tojo-Hideki">

                        </a>
                        (Japan).

                     <h3 class="accordion--question font-14 font-weight-normal cursor-pointer rounded">
                      <div class="pe-none d-flex justify-content-between align-items-center">
                       <div>
                        What were the turning points of World War II?
                       </div>
                       <em class="material-icons" data-icon="expand_more">
                       </em>
                      </div>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="accordion--answer hidden p-15 pt-5 font-16 mb-5">
                      <div class="accordion--answer-copy">
                       <p>
                        The
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pacific-War">
                         war in the Pacific
                        </a>
                        turned against Japan during the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Midway">
                         Battle of Midway
                        </a>
                        (June 3–6, 1942), an American victory that destroyed the Japanese first-line
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/aircraft-carrier">
                         carrier
                        </a>
                        force and, together with the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Guadalcanal">
                         Battle of Guadalcanal
                        </a>
                        , ended Japan’s ability to prosecute an offensive war.
                       </p>
                       <p>
                        The tide of the war in Europe shifted with the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union">
                         Soviet
                        </a>
                        victory at the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Stalingrad">
                         Battle of Stalingrad
                        </a>
                        (February 1943). More than one million Soviet troops and tens of thousands of civilians died in the defense of the city, but the destruction of two entire German armies marked the beginning of the end of the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Third-Reich">
                         Third Reich
                        </a>
                        .
                       </p>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                    </div>

                       <div>
                        How did World War II end?
                       </div>
                       <em class="material-icons" data-icon="expand_more">
                       </em>
                      </div>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="accordion--answer hidden p-15 pt-5 font-16 mb-5">
                      <div class="accordion--answer-copy">
                       <p>
                        The
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Allied-Powers-international-alliance">
                         Allied
                        </a>
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/Normandy-Invasion">
                         landings at Normandy
                        </a>
                        on June 6, 1944, opened a second front in Europe, and Germany’s abortive
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-the-Bulge">
                         offensive at the Ardennes
                        </a>
                        in the winter of 1944–45 marked the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Third-Reich">
                         Third Reich
                        </a>
                        ’s final push in the west. The
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Red-Army">
                         Red Army
                        </a>
                        advanced from the east and effectively claimed all the territory under its control for the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Soviet-Union">
                         Soviet
                        </a>
                        sphere. The Allied armies converged on Berlin.
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adolf-Hitler">
                         Adolf Hitler
                        </a>
                        committed suicide on April 30, 1945, and the war in Europe ended on May 8.
                       </p>
                       <p>
                        The American “island hopping” campaign had destroyed key Japanese installations throughout the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pacific-War">
                         Pacific
                        </a>
                        while allowing bypassed islands to wither on the vine. Hundreds of thousands were killed in firebombings of Japanese cities, and the
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/atomic-bombings-of-Hiroshima-and-Nagasaki">
                         atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki
                        </a>
                        in August 1945 knocked Japan out of the war.
                       </p>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                    </div>

                     <h3 class="accordion--question font-14 font-weight-normal cursor-pointer rounded">
                      <div class="pe-none d-flex justify-content-between align-items-center">
                       <div>
                        How many people died during World War II?
                       </div>
                       <em class="material-icons" data-icon="expand_more">
                       </em>

                     <div class="accordion--answer hidden p-15 pt-5 font-16 mb-5">
                      <div class="accordion--answer-copy">
                       <p>
                        Estimates of the total number of people killed during World War II have ranged from 35,000,000 to 60,000,000—a significant span, because statistics about the war’s casualties are inexact. The Soviet Union and China are believed to have suffered the most total casualties, while an estimated 5,800,000 Poles died, which represents about 20 percent of Poland’s prewar population. About 4,200,000 Germans died, and about 1,972,000 Japanese died. In all, the scale of human losses during World War II was vast. A table that details estimated deaths by country is available
                        <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II/Costs-of-the-war">

                    ,  conflict that involved virtually every part of the world during the years 1939–45. The
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/war">

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Axis-Powers">

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany">

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy">
                     Italy

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Britain-island-Europe">

                    </a>

                    </span>

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/total-war">

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/strategic-bombing-during-World-War-II">

                    </a>

                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="ideology" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ideology">

                   <section data-level="2" id="ref53532">
                    <h2 class="h2">
                     The outbreak of war
                    </h2>
                    <!--[PREMOD4]-->
                    <span class="marker PREMOD4 mod-inline">
                    </span>
                    <p class="topic-paragraph">
                     By the early part of 1939 the
                     <span id="ref511767">
                     </span>
                     <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany">
                      German
                     </a>
                     dictator
                     <span id="ref511768">
                     </span>
                     <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Adolf-Hitler">
                      Adolf Hitler
                     </a>
                     had become determined to invade and occupy
                     <span id="ref511769">
                     </span>
                     <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Poland">
                      Poland
                     </a>
                     . Poland, for its part, had guarantees of French and British military support should it be attacked by Germany. Hitler intended to invade Poland anyway, but first he had to neutralize the possibility that the Soviet Union would resist the invasion of its western neighbor. Secret negotiations led on August 23–24 to the signing of the
                     <span id="ref511770">
                     </span>
                     <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/German-Soviet-Nonaggression-Pact">
                      German-Soviet Nonaggression Pact
                     </a>
                     in
                     <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Moscow">
                      Moscow
                     </a>
                     . In a secret
                     <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="protocol" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/protocol">
                      protocol
                     </a>
                     of this pact, the Germans and the Soviets agreed that Poland should be divided between them, with the western third of the country going to Germany and the eastern two-thirds being taken over by the U.S.S.R.
                    </p>
                    <div class="js-qf-module qf-module px-40 px-sm-20 py-15 mx-auto module-spacing font-14 bg-gray-50 rounded">

                    <p class="topic-paragraph">
                     Having achieved this
                     <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="cynical" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cynical">
                      cynical
                     </a>
                     agreement, the other provisions of which stupefied Europe even without divulgence of the secret protocol, Hitler thought that Germany could attack Poland with no danger of Soviet or British intervention and gave orders for the invasion to start on August 26. News of the signing, on August 25, of a formal treaty of mutual assistance between Great Britain and Poland (to supersede a previous though temporary agreement) caused him to postpone the start of hostilities for a few days. He was still determined, however, to ignore the diplomatic efforts of the western powers to restrain him. Finally, at 12:40
                     <span class="text-smallcaps">
                      pm
                     </span>
                     on August 31, 1939, Hitler ordered hostilities against Poland to start at 4:45 the next morning. The invasion began as ordered. In response,
                     <span id="ref511771">
                     </span>
                     <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/United-Kingdom">
                      Great Britain
                     </a>
                     and
                     <span id="ref511772">
                     </span>
                     <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/France">
                      France
                     </a>
                     declared war on Germany on September 3, at 11:00
                     <span class="text-smallcaps">
                      am
                     </span>
                     and at 5:00
                     <span class="text-smallcaps">
                      pm
                     </span>
                     , respectively. World War II had begun.
                    </p>
